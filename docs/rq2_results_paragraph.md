# RQ2 Results Paragraph

For RQ2, we evaluated whether QUBO-graph-aware variable ordering and topology-aware logical mapping could reduce transpilation overhead relative to natural, random, and standard Qiskit baselines. The full RQ2 experiment reused the 60-instance extended QUBO dataset and evaluated three sparse coupling-map topologies: line, two-dimensional grid, and heavy-hex-like sparse. Eight mapping strategies were compared, including standard Qiskit layout/routing, natural fixed layout, random fixed relabeling, degree-based fixed relabeling, weighted-degree-based fixed relabeling, degree-based centrality placement, weighted-degree centrality placement, and BFS-based topology-aware placement. This produced 1,440 strategy-topology-instance observations.

The results indicate that standard Qiskit layout/routing is a strong baseline. Relative to the natural fixed layout, standard Qiskit reduced transpiled depth by an average of 7.36 gates and reduced both two-qubit count and SWAP count by an average of 12.26 operations. Its average percentage reductions were approximately 8.4% for depth, 8.6% for two-qubit count, and 33.3% for SWAP count. Standard Qiskit also achieved the highest win rates against the natural fixed layout, improving two-qubit and SWAP counts in approximately 71.1% of cases. These results show that any proposed QUBO-graph-aware mapping strategy must be evaluated against a strong transpiler baseline rather than only against a fixed natural layout.

In contrast, the custom graph-aware and topology-aware strategies did not provide a universal improvement. Degree-based and weighted-degree-based fixed relabeling increased mean transpiled depth and routing overhead relative to the natural fixed layout. Random fixed relabeling was even less stable, producing larger average increases in depth, two-qubit count, and SWAP count. The centrality-based and BFS-based topology-aware heuristics also increased overhead on average, indicating that naive attempts to place graph-important variables on central physical qubits or to match BFS orderings do not reliably reduce routing cost. These findings are consistent with the pilot diagnostics: graph-aware ordering can improve selected scheduling-derived cases, but the effect is not robust across QUBO families and topologies.

Family- and topology-level summaries further suggest that mapping effects are structure-dependent. Assignment-style QUBOs were relatively insensitive to degree-based relabeling because their constraint-induced interaction structure often preserves symmetry under such reorderings. Scheduling-derived toy QUBOs showed selected cases where degree-based ordering reduced overhead, whereas MaxCut-style instances were more likely to be harmed by simple graph-aware relabeling. Similarly, the penalty for poor mapping was most visible on sparse and path-like topologies, where physical locality constraints make routing more sensitive to the placement of interacting variables.

Overall, RQ2 does not support a universal claim that QUBO-graph-aware variable ordering or simple topology-aware placement reduces transpilation overhead. Instead, the results support a more bounded and practically important conclusion: mapping strategy performance is baseline-dependent, family-dependent, and topology-dependent. Standard Qiskit layout/routing is a strong default baseline, random ordering is unstable, and naive graph-aware or topology-aware heuristics can increase routing overhead. Therefore, QUBO-graph-aware compilation should be reported as a strategy-dependent readiness profile rather than as a universal mapping rule.
