# RQ3 Results Paragraph

For RQ3, we evaluated how sub-QUBO extraction strategies trade off interaction preservation, solution-quality preservation, and QAOA circuit executability. The full RQ3 analysis used all extended QUBO instances with 24 and 32 variables, covering MaxCut-style, assignment-style, and scheduling-derived toy QUBOs. Four extraction strategies were compared: top-weight-edge extraction, high-degree-node extraction, weighted-degree-node extraction, and random-node extraction. Subproblem sizes of k = 8, 12, and 16 variables were evaluated. For each extracted sub-QUBO, we measured interaction preservation, transpilation savings relative to the original QUBO, and lifted solution quality relative to a classical local-search reference on the original QUBO.

The results show a clear preservation–executability trade-off. Across the full RQ3 dataset, weighted-degree-node extraction preserved the largest fraction of weighted interactions, with a mean weighted-edge preservation ratio of 0.367. High-degree-node extraction was similar, with a mean ratio of 0.360. Top-weight-edge extraction preserved a lower but still substantial fraction of weighted interactions, with a mean ratio of 0.332. Random-node extraction preserved substantially less weighted interaction structure, with a mean ratio of 0.214. These results indicate that degree- and weighted-degree-based node selection better preserves the original QUBO interaction structure than random extraction.

However, interaction preservation did not align monotonically with circuit-executability gains. Top-weight-edge extraction produced the largest mean depth reduction relative to the original QUBO circuits, approximately 46.3%, and also reduced two-qubit count and SWAP count by approximately 73.4% and 89.3%, respectively. Random-node extraction produced similarly large circuit reductions, with approximately 44.9% depth reduction, 81.6% two-qubit reduction, and 89.6% SWAP reduction, but did so while preserving the least weighted interaction structure. In contrast, weighted-degree-node extraction preserved the most interaction weight but produced smaller, though still substantial, executability gains: approximately 39.7% depth reduction, 69.0% two-qubit reduction, and 83.0% SWAP reduction. This pattern illustrates the central RQ3 trade-off: more aggressive or less structure-preserving extraction can make QAOA circuits more executable, but at the cost of discarding more of the original QUBO interaction structure.

The subproblem size parameter controlled this trade-off directly. For k = 8, mean weighted-edge preservation ranged from approximately 0.128 to 0.213 across extraction strategies, while mean two-qubit reductions ranged from approximately 83.3% to 89.9% and mean SWAP reductions ranged from approximately 95.2% to 98.1%. For k = 12, weighted-edge preservation increased to approximately 0.165–0.362, while mean two-qubit and SWAP reductions decreased to approximately 69.8–86.5% and 85.1–92.7%, respectively. For k = 16, weighted-edge preservation further increased to approximately 0.350–0.527, while mean two-qubit and SWAP reductions decreased to approximately 53.1–68.5% and 67.7–79.6%, respectively. Thus, increasing sub-QUBO size preserves more of the original interaction graph but reduces the executability gains obtained from extraction.

Solution-quality preservation did not follow interaction preservation monotonically. Across the full RQ3 dataset, random-node extraction had the lowest mean normalized lifted-solution gap, but it also preserved the least interaction structure, indicating that this result should not be interpreted as random extraction being generally preferable. Instead, solution-quality preservation was family-dependent and sensitive to the fill-zero lifting rule used to evaluate sub-QUBO solutions in the original variable space. In MaxCut-style instances, weighted-degree-node extraction produced the smallest mean normalized quality gap, whereas assignment-style and scheduling-derived toy instances exhibited different rankings. These results indicate that interaction preservation, executability, and lifted solution quality are related but distinct dimensions of sub-QUBO readiness.

Overall, RQ3 supports the premise that sub-QUBO extraction should be treated as a multi-objective readiness trade-off rather than as a single best extraction rule. Smaller sub-QUBOs improve QAOA circuit executability but sacrifice interaction preservation; larger sub-QUBOs preserve more original structure but reduce compilation savings. Solution-quality preservation further depends on QUBO family, extraction strategy, and the lifting rule used for evaluation. These findings reinforce the paper's readiness-profile framing: subproblem extraction can make QUBO-derived QAOA circuits more executable, but the resulting subproblem should be reported in terms of preservation, executability, and lifted solution-quality metrics rather than as a universally optimal reduction.
