# RQ1 Extended Results Paragraph

For the extended RQ1 analysis, we evaluated 60 QUBO instances from three families: MaxCut-style, assignment-style, and scheduling-derived toy QUBOs. The instances covered five variable sizes, from 8 to 32 binary variables, and were compiled as p = 1 QAOA-style circuits under four local coupling-map topologies: fully connected, two-dimensional grid, heavy-hex-like sparse, and line. This produced 240 topology-specific transpilation observations. The results show that QUBO interaction-graph structure provides a strong first-order explanation of QAOA circuit complexity. A compact regression model for log-transformed pre-transpilation depth using graph descriptors achieved an adjusted R-squared of 0.979, indicating that graph-structural descriptors captured most of the topology-independent circuit-depth variation.

Post-transpilation results further showed that topology-aware descriptors add explanatory value beyond graph descriptors and topology fixed effects. For log-transformed transpiled depth, a graph-descriptor model with topology fixed effects achieved an adjusted R-squared of 0.948. Adding topology-alignment descriptors increased the adjusted R-squared to 0.966. In this model, topology-alignment ratio was negatively associated with transpiled depth, while weighted mean topology distance was positively associated with transpiled depth. This pattern is consistent with the expected routing interpretation: QUBO interactions that are more directly aligned with the coupling map require less routing, whereas interactions spanning longer topology distances increase circuit depth.

The topology-alignment pattern was especially clear for SWAP insertion. For log-transformed SWAP count across all topologies, the graph-and-alignment model achieved an adjusted R-squared of 0.951. Because the fully connected topology has zero SWAP count by construction, we also fitted a sparse-topology-only model, which achieved an adjusted R-squared of 0.964. In both SWAP-count models, topology-alignment ratio was negatively associated with SWAP count, whereas weighted mean topology distance was positively associated with SWAP count. These findings support the RQ1 premise that QUBO graph descriptors explain baseline QAOA circuit complexity, while QUBO-topology alignment descriptors explain additional topology-aware transpilation overhead.

Although interaction-edge count showed strong positive marginal associations with circuit depth in the correlation screening, multivariable regression coefficients should be interpreted conditionally because several graph descriptors are correlated. In the regression models, max degree and coefficient-distribution descriptors captured substantial conditional variation once edge count, density, family, topology, and alignment descriptors were included. Therefore, the RQ1 conclusion is not that a single descriptor universally determines circuit complexity, but that a compact profile of QUBO graph structure and topology alignment provides a useful explanatory basis for predicting QAOA compilation outcomes. These results are compilation-level evidence only and do not imply quantum advantage, QAOA optimization superiority, or hardware execution performance.
